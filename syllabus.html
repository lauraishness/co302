Course Overview

"We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done." -- ALAN TURING

CO302, like other 300-level composition courses, emphasizes writing processes with specific attention to revising and editing, and critical reading processes with attention to reading from various points of view. We diverge from other 300-level courses with our emphasis on experiencing and producing primarily digital texts.

To achieve this, you will explore the rhetorical and cultural contexts in which electronic texts occur, and practice strategies for interpreting and producing these texts. You need not be (and will not become) expert designers and coders in this course, as these are professions in their own right that one 16-week course cannot equip you for; however, my hope is that you develop technical literacies and rhetorical competencies that help you navigate a world where information and ideas are shared and produced in online, digital contexts.

Guiding Questions

  • What are the affordances of various online spaces over non-digital print forms of publication? What even is a text? How does digital production expand our notions of textuality?
  • How does the participatory nature of online discourse affect its authors, audiences, and contexts? How are texts and images distributed in networks?
  • How do we, as consumers of digital/online texts, determine the reliability & usability of what we read, see, and hear online?
  • As producers of digital texts, what strategies and skills do we need to know, technically and rhetorically, for effective communication?
  • What rights and responsibilities do we have as users & producers?

Course Overview Continued...

In order to compose, we will learn some basic literacies with HTML/CSS, photo editing, document design, and other technologies; however, I encourage you to self-advocate and experiment with mediums that interest you, knowing that your technical competency is not the primary learning outcome or evaluative criteria. Use the instigators, the homework, and the larger projects as "sand-box" type projects as far as medium and technology goes.

  • Have you always wanted to start a blog? Develop a professional online portfolio? You'll get the chance here.
  • Want to work on your video editing skills in a low-stakes context? Go for it.
  • Want a space to learn & practice audio production? Let's hear it.
  • Want to practice coding in a new language or integrating with a new platform? Try here.

Know that your success in this course depends on your ability to employ rhetorical strategies, think deeply & critically, and put forth a genuine effort to learn something new that excites you; you are not evaluated on your ability to become completely literate in the mediums or technologies you choose to use. Rather, we will learn to think rhetorically about the technologies & texts we encounter and use.

Written Communication Competency―Criteria for Written Communication

Competency in written communication is a student’s ability to write and express ideas across a variety of genres and styles. Written communication abilities develop over time through layered, interactive, and continual processes and experiences across the curriculum.

Student Learning Outcomes (SLOs) Students should be able to:

Employ Rhetorical Knowledge
Exhibit a thorough understanding of audience, purpose, genre, and context that is responsive to the situation.
Develop Content
Create and develop ideas within the context of the situation and the assigned task(s).
Apply Genre & Disciplinary Conventions
Apply formal and informal conventions of writing, including organization, content, presentation, formatting, and stylistic choices, in particular forms and/or fields.
Use Sources & Evidence
a. Critically read, evaluate, apply, and synthesize evidence and/or sources in support of a claim. b. Follow an appropriate documentation system.
Control Syntax and Mechanics
Demonstrate proficiency with conventions, including spellings, grammar, mechanics, and word choice appropriate to the writing task.

Advanced Writing Course (GT-CO3)―Content Criteria

Extend Rhetorical Knowledge

  • Use texts from rhetoric, discourse studies, communication, or related disciplines to extend understanding of rhetorical concepts to the discipline that is the focus of the course.
  • Develop sophisticated strategies for critical analysis of disciplinary or specialized discourse.
  • Learn more sophisticated ways to communicate knowledge to appropriate audiences.
  • Apply reflective strategies to the synthesis, communication, and creation of knowledge.

Extend Experience in Writing

  • Hone recursive strategies for generating ideas, revising, editing, and proofreading for disciplinary or specialized discourse.
  • Critique one’s own and other’s work, including the work of professional writers and/or scholars.

Extend Critical and Creative Thinking

  • Reflect on the implications and consequences of context.
  • Incorporate alternate, divergent or contradictory perspectives or ideas within one’s own position.
  • Extend and complicate the consequences of the stated conclusion.

Use Sources and Evidence

  • Select, evaluate, and synthesize appropriate sources and evidence.
  • Use discipline-appropriate criteria to evaluate sources and evidence.

Extend Application of Composing Conventions

  • Select and adapt genre conventions including structure, paragraphing, tone, mechanics, syntax, and style for disciplinary or specialized discourse.
  • Use specialized vocabulary, format, and documentation appropriately in more extensive or in-depth writing objects.

GRADES & ASSIGNMENTS

Any changes to the value or due date of assignments will be announced in class and online. Instructor reserves the right to make changes to assignments based on the needs of the class.  

You’ll receive an assignment guide with each major assignment that I’ll use to assess the project. Generally, each assignment is assessed using the hierarchy of rhetorical concerns. Carefully prepare & proofread your final draft submissions. Make sure each submission is given a file name that suites your audience (i.e. “comphw.docx” is too vague). MLA citation format must be used for all applicable assignments.

Due Dates

Deadlines mean that major assignments must be submitted to Canvas or sent to me by email by the designated time (on the assignment sheet or canvas) on the due date. I do not accept late major assignments for full credit (half a letter grade is deducted every 24 hours the project is late); because of a large course load with many students, I cannot accept late process work (homework, workshop drafts, smaller assignments) at all. Major assignments submitted more than 5 days after the due date will not be accepted.

Due dates for homework, instigators, and other smaller assignments will be announced in class or via canvas or the course schedules. You are responsible for keeping track of all due dates for the course. Do reach out, though, if you are ever confused about a due date; not knowing a due date (or confusion about a due date) does not override the due date. Please self-advocate and stay on top of assignments by using the many course resources at your disposal.

Extensions & Extenuating Circumstances

In general, I do not grant exceptions to late work in retrospect; however, if you have an extenuating circumstance arise that interferes with your ability to do your best work on a major assignment, I will consider an extension if you contact me via email in advance of the due date. Requests for extensions after a due date has passed will not be granted. These extensions apply to major projects; unfortunately I do not have the capacity to provide extensions for homework, workshops, instigators, or other smaller assignments.

Expectation of Workload

Please keep in mind that the University expects homework or other work outside of instructional time to be equivalent to the federal credit hour definition of at least 2 hours of outside work for each contact hour.

Major Assignments

Assignment 1 / Analysis of a Discourse Community / 120 pts

Identify and analyze an online discourse community of your choice. This can be a discourse community that you participate in (or have participated in the past), one that you know about through peers, or somewhere you’ve never explored before. Dig deeply into this rhetorical situation to get beyond the surface of this community’s purpose, audience, power dynamics, conventions, and quirks. Read more on the assignment page (link forthcoming).

Assignment 2 / Website Draft & Reflection / 100 pts

Create a focused website draft, designed with either bootstrap themes, by hand, or with another WYSIWYG site (Wix, wordpress, weebly, etc.) You will automatically receive +5 bonus points if you attempt either a code-edited bootstrap/HTML5up theme or code from scratch. After you have completed your website, draft a 1-2 page (~500 words) reflection that considers the rhetorical choices you've made with the content & design. Read more on the assignment page (link forthcoming).

Assignment 3 / Research Proposal & Action Plan / 100 pts

Propose an idea for A4's Longform project and complete initial research (5 sources) for the idea. You may complete A3 & A4 individually, or as a partner/co-authored project. After outlining the proposed idea for A4, follow up with an action plan of how you will complete this long form project. Read more on the assignment page (link forthcoming).

Assignment 4 / Long Form Multimodal Project / 250 pts

A4 is the largest project this semester; as such, there are many directions you can take this long form multimodal project about an issue or topic of your choice. You may complete A3 & A4 individually, or as a partner/co-authored project. Regardless of the option you choose, you will analyze, research, and author a longer-form multimodal project and briefly present your project to the class during week 15 or finals week. Read more on the assignment page (link forthcoming).

Final Presentation of Long Form Multimodal Project / 50 pts

During week 15 & finals week, individuals or partners will present their A4 projects to the class. You may complete A3 & A4 individually, or as a partner/co-authored project. Regardless of the option you choose, you will analyze, research, and author a longer-form multimodal project and briefly present your project to the class during week 15 or finals week. Instigators are written or multi-modal responses to the readings. What did the reading make you do or think? What's one way to apply the reading? What's your critique or reaction to the reading? Instigators give you a chance to apply and react to the texts that we're reading in this course -- they might also help form our course discussions on Wednesdays.

I'm hesitant to put any parameters on these -- because I want you to dig deep and be creative. Instigators are also a chance to use a medium you don't normally use, to practice creating online content, and to think about the implications of the ideas we're exploring in the course.

My only stipulations:

  • Each instigator response should reflect genuine effort / tie back to the readings in some way; synthesize direct quotations or paraphrases (attribute!!) in your responses.
  • See Course resources for a few examples (I'll be adding to this list)
  • Contain 1-2 questions that you want us to discuss in class
  • Complete 6 of the 12 (3 of which MUST be multimodal responses, i.e., not just a text-based answer)
  • Must be emailed to the professor (laura.price@colostate.edu) by Wednesday at 9am for MWF or Tuesday by 8am for TTh section. Late submissions will not be considered.

Remember that these instigators are worth 18% of your grade; there will be 12 opportunities for instigators -- you must complete 6, and you may complete up to 2 additional for extra credit.

3 of these instigators must use a digital medium other than a simple essay format (meaning you may make a blog post, a podcast, a youtube video, a Photo-essay, a post on social media, a flyer/poster, etc. etc.) Your instigator should also contain 1-2 questions about the readings that you’d like to pose to the class. At the beginning of our Wednesday discussion, we will take a poll on which questions we want to focus on for the discussion.

Each instigator is worth up to 30 pts. Occasionally, bonus points are granted for exceptional execution, creativity, or depth of thought.

ATTENDANCE & MAKE-UP WORK

The CSU Composition Program knows that consistent attendance and regular participation are essential components in composition courses. We emphasize the writing process as being just as important as the final written product.

Therefore, when you are not present in class, you cannot participate in required class discussion, workshops, group work, and assignments. According to the CSU General Catalog, “Students should attend all classes for which they are registered to obtain maximum educational benefits. Absence or lateness does not excuse students from required course work.”

Your attendance is crucial to your success in this course and contributes to the learning environment. Missing class often means you'll miss out on important instructions for major assignments, and on graded in-class discussions & writings. If you know you will be absent, you should plan to submit your work early. If you have an extenuating circumstance, you should speak with me in a timely fashion.

If you are absent, you cannot make up any in-class work we did that day (e.g. group work,  discussion, quizzes, in-class writing activities, workshop, etc.) unless you make prior arrangements by contacting me via email at least 1 week in advance.

PLAGIARISM & ACADEMIC HONESTY

Plagiarism will not be tolerated in this class. Any instance of plagiarism will result in a reduced or failing grade of the assignment, failure of the course, and/or removal of the Repeat/Delete option for the course. Ignorance is not an excuse for “accidental” plagiarism.

The following are examples of plagiarism (though this list is not exhaustive):

  • Submitting someone else’s work as your own;
  • Submitting a paper that was purchased or downloaded from the internet;
  • Using someone else’s ideas, language, or structure and not properly attributing them;
  • Using a phrase (whether quotation or paraphrase) without acknowledging its source;
  • Using a source without citing it correctly;
  • “Padding” a bibliography by making up sources or citing a source you didn’t use in your research. 

This class adheres to the Academic Integrity Policy of the General Catalog and the Student Code of Conduct.  Please see these documents for further details. In cases of suspected academic dishonesty, all instructors at CSU are required to forward documentary evidence to the Office of Conflict Resolution and Student Conduct Services.  This office will ensure due process and advise instructors regarding appropriate disposition of the case.
